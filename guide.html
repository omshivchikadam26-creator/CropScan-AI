What is Plant Disease?

Plant disease is any abnormality in plant function or structure caused by biological agents — fungi, bacteria, viruses, or nematodes — or abiotic factors such as nutrient deficiency, drought, or temperature extremes.

Early identification is critical. Once a pathogen establishes itself, it can spread exponentially, wiping out entire fields within days under the right conditions.

Why Early Detection Matters

Many diseases begin asymptomatically. By the time visible symptoms appear, the infection has often spread to neighbouring tissue. Early-stage detection can reduce crop loss by up to 70%.

AI-based image diagnosis can detect sub-visual patterns in leaf colouration and texture that the human eye misses, enabling interventions days before lesions appear.

Common Diseases — Quick Reference

Early Blight Alternaria solani (Fungus) Tomato, Potato Dark concentric rings on older leaves; yellowing around lesions Copper fungicide, crop rotation, remove infected leaves

Bacterial Spot Xanthomonas spp. (Bacteria) Tomato, Peach, Pepper Small, water-soaked spots; lesions turn brown and fall out Copper hydroxide spray; avoid overhead irrigation

Prevention Best Practices

  • Rotate crops every season to break disease cycles in the soil.
  • Use certified disease-free seeds and transplants from reputable sources.
  • Water at soil level in the morning to minimise foliar wetness.
  • Maintain adequate plant spacing for air circulation — poor airflow drives fungal outbreaks.
  • Apply preventative neem oil or copper spray every 3–4 weeks during high-risk periods.
  • Remove and destroy (never compost) infected plant material promptly.
  • Monitor fields weekly using a systematic grid pattern for early-stage detection.
  • Disinfect tools with 70% isopropyl alcohol between plants to prevent transfer.
🌡

Environmental Risk Factors

Disease pressure increases dramatically under specific environmental conditions. Understanding these helps time preventive sprays and reduce losses.

High humidity (above 80%) — primary driver of fungal and bacterial spread. Ensure drainage and airflow.

Temperature range 15–25°C — optimal germination window for most fungal spores.

Leaf wetness duration — even 6 hours of continuous moisture can trigger Late Blight in tomato. Post-rain inspections are essential.

Dense canopy — restricts airflow and traps humidity close to leaf surfaces. Prune regularly.
